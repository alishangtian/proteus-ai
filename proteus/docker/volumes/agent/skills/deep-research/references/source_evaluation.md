# Source Evaluation Criteria

This guide provides comprehensive criteria and techniques for evaluating the credibility, reliability, and relevance of information sources during deep research.

## Source Type Classification

### Primary Sources
- **Definition**: Original, first-hand accounts or data
- **Examples**: Research papers, official documents, raw datasets, interviews, original surveys
- **Evaluation Focus**: Methodology, data collection techniques, potential biases in collection

### Secondary Sources
- **Definition**: Analysis, interpretation, or synthesis of primary sources
- **Examples**: Review articles, textbooks, news analyses, industry reports
- **Evaluation Focus**: Author expertise, analytical rigor, citation of primary sources

### Tertiary Sources
- **Definition**: Compilations or summaries of secondary sources
- **Examples**: Encyclopedias, dictionaries, handbooks, some websites
- **Evaluation Focus**: Recency, comprehensiveness, editorial oversight

## Credibility Assessment Framework

### 1. Authority Evaluation

**Author/Organization Credentials:**
- **Academic**: Institutional affiliation, publication record, citations
- **Industry**: Company position, years of experience, professional recognition
- **Media**: Journalist expertise, outlet reputation, editorial standards

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Author name and credentials clearly stated
- [ ] Organizational affiliation provided
- [ ] Contact information available
- [ ] Author expertise matches subject matter
- [ ] No conflict of interest apparent

### 2. Accuracy Verification

**Fact-Checking Methods:**
- Cross-reference with 2+ independent sources
- Verify statistics with original datasets
- Check calculation methods for numerical claims
- Look for contradictory evidence

**Red Flags:**
- Unsupported extraordinary claims
- Contradictions within the source itself
- Logical fallacies in arguments
- Misuse or misinterpretation of data

### 3. Currency Assessment

**Timeliness Considerations:**
- **Time-sensitive topics** (technology, markets): Sources < 2 years old
- **Established knowledge** (science, history): Sources < 5-10 years old
- **Historical context**: Older sources may be appropriate for historical analysis

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Publication date clearly stated
- [ ] Last update date provided (for websites)
- [ ] Information reflects current reality
- [ ] References to recent developments included

### 4. Objectivity Analysis

**Bias Detection:**
- **Funding sources**: Corporate sponsorship, government grants, ideological backing
- **Organizational mission**: Advocacy groups vs. neutral research institutions
- **Tone and language**: Emotional language, loaded terms, persuasive intent
- **Balance**: Acknowledgment of opposing views, fair representation

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Potential biases explicitly acknowledged
- [ ] Multiple perspectives presented
- [ ] Evidence supports conclusions
- [ ] Distinction between facts and opinions clear

### 5. Coverage & Depth

**Completeness Assessment:**
- Scope matches research needs
- Depth sufficient for analysis
- Key aspects addressed comprehensively
- Limitations acknowledged

## Source Quality Rating Scale

### Tier 1: Highly Credible
- Peer-reviewed academic journals
- Government statistical agencies
- Reputable international organizations (UN, WHO, IMF)
- Major news outlets with strong fact-checking
- Industry standards organizations

### Tier 2: Generally Reliable
- Established industry publications
- Professional associations
- Well-regarded think tanks
- Company annual reports (SEC filings)
- Technical documentation from vendors

### Tier 3: Variable Reliability
- Personal blogs/websites
- Forums and discussion boards
- Social media content
- Company marketing materials
- Some online encyclopedias

### Tier 4: Low Reliability
- Unverified user-generated content
- Clickbait websites
- Known propaganda outlets
- Sources with consistent fact-checking failures
- AI-generated content without verification

## Domain-Specific Evaluation Guidelines

### Academic/Scientific Sources
- **Impact factor** of journal
- **Citation count** of paper
- **Reproducibility** of findings
- **Methodology** transparency
- **Peer review** status

### News & Media Sources
- **Editorial standards** and corrections policy
- **Journalist expertise** in subject area
- **Source attribution** for claims
- **Balance** in reporting
- **Fact-checking** track record

### Industry & Market Reports
- **Research methodology** description
- **Sample size** and representativeness
- **Data collection** timeframe
- **Sponsorship** disclosure
- **Analyst credentials**

### Government & Official Sources
- **Agency mandate** and expertise
- **Data collection** methodology
- **Transparency** of processes
- **Political context** considerations
- **Update frequency**

## Verification Techniques

### Multi-Source Corroboration
1. Identify key claims requiring verification
2. Search for 2-3 independent sources making same claim
3. Check if sources reference each other (circular reporting)
4. Look for consensus among credible sources

### Original Source Tracing
1. Follow citations to original research
2. Verify statistics with primary datasets
3. Check context of quotes or excerpts
4. Confirm translations accuracy for foreign sources

### Expert Consensus Assessment
1. Identify leading experts in the field
2. Review their public statements or publications
3. Check professional consensus statements
4. Note areas of disagreement among experts

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### 1. Confirmation Bias
- Seeking only information that confirms existing beliefs
- Dismissing contradictory evidence without evaluation

### 2. Authority Bias
- Over-relying on prestigious sources without critical evaluation
- Assuming credentials guarantee accuracy in all domains

### 3. Recency Bias
- Overvaluing recent information while dismissing older but still valid information
- Assuming newer automatically means better or more accurate

### 4. Availability Bias
- Relying on easily accessible information
- Overweighting memorable or dramatic examples

## Source Evaluation Checklist

For each significant source, answer:

**Authority**
- [ ] Who created this content? What are their credentials?
- [ ] Is the creator qualified to speak on this topic?
- [ ] What organization publishes this content?

**Accuracy**
- [ ] Is the information supported by evidence?
- [ ] Are sources cited for factual claims?
- [ ] Can facts be verified elsewhere?

**Currency**
- [ ] When was this published/updated?
- [ ] Is the information current enough for the topic?
- [ ] Are links/references up-to-date?

**Objectivity**
- [ ] What is the purpose of this content?
- [ ] Are biases clearly stated or apparent?
- [ ] Are multiple perspectives presented?

**Coverage**
- [ ] Does this address the topic comprehensively?
- [ ] What is included/excluded from coverage?
- [ ] Are limitations acknowledged?
